HIS POLITICAL EXPERIENCE

Exposure to Political Ideologies


Fela Kuti's political awakening began during his visit to the United States in the late 1960s. There, he encountered the Black Power movement and civil rights activism, which profoundly influenced his worldview. He met Sandra Smith, a Black Panther activist, who introduced him to Pan-Africanist ideologies and the writings of Malcolm X. This exposure motivated Fela to use his music as a tool for political and social change.


Music as a Weapon of Resistance


Returning to Nigeria, Fela transformed his music into a platform for political commentary. He addressed themes such as corruption, police brutality, inequality, and the failures of the Nigerian government. Songs like "Zombie" mocked the Nigerian military’s blind obedience, while "Sorrow, Tears, and Blood" highlighted the repression faced by ordinary citizens. His music became anthems of resistance, resonating with marginalized communities.


The Kalakuta Republic


Fela established the Kalakuta Republic, a self-declared independent state, to symbolize his rejection of Nigeria’s corrupt leadership. The commune housed his band, family, and political allies, and it became a hub for activism. Kalakuta often faced attacks from the government, with the most notable being a 1977 military raid that destroyed the commune and led to the death of Fela's mother.


Presidential Ambitions and Party Formation


In 1979, Fela founded the Movement of the People (MOP), a political party aimed at representing the interests of ordinary Nigerians. He declared his intention to run for president in Nigeria’s elections, advocating for anti-corruption, social justice, and Pan-African unity. However, his candidacy was rejected by the authorities, further highlighting the systemic oppression he fought against.


Legacy as a Political Activist


Fela’s political experience was marked by frequent arrests, harassment, and physical assaults, yet he remained unwavering in his activism. He used his music and platform to challenge the status quo, inspiring Nigerians to demand accountability from their leaders. Today, Fela is remembered not only as a musical legend but also as a fearless activist who stood for justice and freedom, leaving a lasting impact on Nigerian politics and society.
